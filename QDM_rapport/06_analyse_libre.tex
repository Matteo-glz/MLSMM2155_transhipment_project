\subsection{Motivation and Scenario Design}
\label{sec:s4_motivation}

Phase~2 subjects the GlobalFlow network to six disruption scenarios
drawn from the assignment specification. These scenarios cover a
broad typological range of tariff shocks, energy crises, node loss and
corridor blockades, but share a common geographic focus on the East
Asian hub cluster and the transatlantic/transpacific trade lanes.
One structural blind spot remains: the network's dependence on the
Suez Canal, the single most critical maritime chokepoint for
GlobalFlow's Europe--Asia and Europe--MiddleEast flows.

\paragraph{Real-world motivation.}
The 2023--2024 Houthi attack campaign on Red Sea shipping is the
largest disruption to Suez traffic in recorded history. By early 2024,
more than 50\% of normal Suez container volumes had diverted onto the
Cape of Good Hope route, adding roughly 9\,000\,km and 10--14 transit
days per voyage \cite{drewry2024}. Crucially, the crisis combined
two economically distinct mechanisms: (i)~a sharp cost increase
as carriers rerouted around the Cape under elevated war-risk insurance
premiums, and (ii)~a partial throughput reduction on the residual
corridor, as the vessels that continued transiting under naval escort
faced delays and congestion. Scenario~S4 replicates this
dual-mechanism structure.

\paragraph{Arc identification.}
Affected arcs are identified dynamically at runtime using the
zone-pair geography embedded in the instance. All sea-mode arcs
crossing the following zone pairs are flagged as Suez-dependent:
Europe$\leftrightarrow$Asia, Europe$\leftrightarrow$MiddleEast.
This yields \textbf{39~sea arcs}. The identification is performed
programmatically (no hardcoded arc list), making it robust to future
instance changes. An assertion verifies mutual exclusivity with any
Panama-corridor set.

\paragraph{Shock parameters.}
S4 applies both mechanisms simultaneously:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Cost factor $\times 1.80$}: variable cost of all
    39~Suez arcs raised by $+80\%$, reflecting the longer Cape of
    Good Hope rerouting distance and elevated insurance.
  \item \textbf{Capacity factor $\times 0.40$}: shared capacity of
    all 39~Suez arcs reduced by $60\%$, reflecting the two-thirds
    of normal Suez traffic that diverts or self-excludes.
\end{itemize}

Both mechanisms are implemented simultaneously; no separate cost-only
or capacity-only variants are reported, as preliminary analysis
(available in the supplementary code) showed that the cost shock
already drives rerouting away from Suez arcs, after which the capacity
constraint is no longer binding --- the combined shock therefore
collapses to the cost-only case in terms of optimal routing, with the
+0.76~pp capacity premium being purely structural.

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:s4_results}

All three strategies are structurally feasible: the $\times 0.40$
capacity reduction, while severe, leaves sufficient residual
throughput once flows are rerouted away from the corridor.
No penalty slack is required; all demand is met.

\subsection{Analysis}
\label{sec:s4_analysis}

\paragraph{Why all three strategies tie.}
The three strategies yield identical costs because the optimal response
to the $+80\%$ cost shock is to reroute all Suez-corridor flows away
from the affected arcs, a purely operational adjustment that
strategy~R can execute without any structural change. Once the solver
avoids those 39 arcs, the $\times 0.40$ capacity constraint on them is
no longer active (the flows have been redirected), so the capacity
shock adds no additional cost. Strategy~A has no profitable new
openings or activations to make. The baseline warehouse footprint
already contains all the intermediate nodes needed for Cape-of-Good-Hope
routing. Strategy~F reaches the same greenfield optimum as R because the
network structure is not the binding constraint; the cost shock is.

This is structurally analogous to scenario~T2 (energy crisis) in
Phase~2, where a uniform per-mode cost multiplier leaves all
alternative paths penalised at least as much as the current ones.
Here, the cost shock affects a specific corridor rather than a
mode, but the effect is the same: rerouting is already
first-best under strategy~R.

\paragraph{Product-level exposure.}
The disruption falls almost entirely on products~B and~C. Product~B
(Semiconductors, $+\$208{,}800$ vs baseline) routes from Singapore via
Europe-bound arcs that transit the Red Sea corridor; the Cape rerouting
adds cost on those legs. Product~C (Battery Components, $+\$134{,}329$)
is similarly exposed on its Europe-bound legs from the Dubai hub.
Product~A (Fertilizers, $+\$930$) is nearly unaffected because its
supply chains are already concentrated in Europe and the Middle East,
with little reliance on the Suez-dependent Asia--Europe arc set.

\paragraph{Positioning within the scenario library.}
Figure~\ref{fig:scenario_ranking} provides the cross-phase ranking.
S4 sits between T2 (energy crisis, $+10.64\%$) and T3 (hub surcharge,
$+19.13\%$) on the feasible disruption scale: at $+7.72\%$ it is more
severe than the T1 tariff shock ($+3.49\%$) and approaches the energy
crisis magnitude. Crucially, it remains well below the topological
shocks of Phase~2: the H3 closure (S1) costs $+81.6\%$, more than
ten times the Suez disruption. This comparison crystallises the
central finding of the entire analysis: \emph{geographic chokepoints
are manageable; topological chokepoints are existential.}

A disruption that raises costs by $+80\%$ on 39~sea arcs propagates as
a $+7.72\%$ system-wide shock because alternative routing exists and
the baseline already uses it efficiently. A disruption that removes a
single hub node propagates as an $+81.6\%$ shock because no
alternative exists for the stranded suppliers. The network is,
in a sense, naturally resilient against corridor-level shocks and
structurally fragile against node-level shocks.

\paragraph{Pre-activation candidates.}
Although the flexibility value is zero under S4, adaptation adds
nothing, the scenario still informs network design. The baseline
routes that become expensive under the Suez shock all transit the same
few warehouse-to-warehouse arcs (notably A199 W4$\rightarrow$W3~sea
and A188 W1$\rightarrow$W12~sea) to access alternative lanes. These
are the same arcs activated under strategy~A in T3 and S1. Their
consistent recurrence across disruption types confirms their role as
structural relief paths. Pre-activating them at baseline would cost
less than \$4{,}000 in fixed costs and would make strategy~A
identical to strategy~R from the first period of any
corridor-level disruption, reducing the adaptation lag to zero.
