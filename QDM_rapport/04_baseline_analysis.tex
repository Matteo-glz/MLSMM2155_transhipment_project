The baseline MIP was solved to global optimality using FICO~Xpress imported as a
Python library with a time limit of 300~seconds.
The solver terminates in less than 0.1~second ($0.024$~s), confirming the
tractability of the model and the suitability of the chosen formulation for the
given instance.

\paragraph{Optimal configuration.}
The solver opens 14 out of 15 warehouses; only warehouse~W9 (Sydney) remains
closed.
Sydney is connected to five hubs and one neighbouring warehouse~(W15~Bangalore),
yet serves a single customer, C26~(Sapporo, Japan), already well covered by
closer Asia-Pacific facilities, making its \$1{,}366 opening cost impossible to
justify.
Of the 47 optional arcs, 11 are activated; all are road connections providing
last-mile delivery from warehouses to nearby customers (arcs A225, A235, A242,
A261, A278, A285, A288, A291, A294, A309, A315).
The optimal cost decomposes as shown in Table~\ref{tab:phase1costs}.

\paragraph{Network structure.}
Hub load is highly asymmetric.
Singapore~(H3) is the dominant transit node: it routes \emph{all} 2{,}222~units
of semiconductor demand~(B) — 100\% of product~B supply — plus 631~units of
battery components~(C), for a combined throughput of 2{,}853~units.
This concentration creates the network's central single point of failure,
discussed further in Phase~2.
Frankfurt~(H1) is the primary hub for fertilisers~(A), handling 1{,}063~units
(51\% of~A demand), while Dubai~(H2) is the only hub active for all three
products simultaneously (325~A, 549~B, 631~C).
Chicago~(H4) and S\~ao Paulo~(H5) serve regional spill-over demand and carry
610 and 1{,}168~units respectively; neither routes semiconductor flow, insulating
them from the H3 vulnerability.

Warehouse utilisation spans a wide range: Rotterdam~(W1) operates at
91.3\%~capacity, while Chennai~(W5) reaches only 4.1\%.
Despite this under-utilisation, W5 remains open because it provides the sole
feasible last-mile connection for one South Indian customer (arc~A315) whose
demand cannot be rerouted without activating a more expensive path.
The three European warehouses~(W1--W3) together absorb most of fertiliser
outflow, consistent with the agricultural-demand concentration in that region.

The cost-minimising solution activates \emph{no air freight}: all flows use
sea transport for long-haul legs and road for last-mile delivery.
Even for semiconductors~(B) — nominally an air-heavy product — the direct
sea connections through H3~(Singapore) are sufficiently cost-competitive under
baseline tariffs.
Sea freight accounts for 79--82\% of flow volume across all three products;
road handles the remaining 18--26\% exclusively in warehouse-to-customer arcs.
This modal choice leaves a substantial latent capacity buffer on air routes,
which becomes relevant when analysing energy-shock scenarios in Phase~2.

\subsection*{Formulation Quality and LP~Relaxation}

\paragraph{LP bound and LP--IP gap.}
We analyse the formulation quality by comparing the LP~relaxation bound~$Z_{\text{LP}}^*$
— obtained by relaxing the binary constraints on $y_w$ and $z_a$ to
$[0,1]$ — against the MIP optimal~$Z^* = \$4{,}299{,}810$.
FICO~Xpress solves the MIP to global optimality at the root node of the B\&B
tree in 0.024~seconds with no branching, implying that the LP~relaxation at the
root is already integer-valued.
The LP--IP gap is therefore \textbf{0\%}: $Z_{\text{LP}}^* = Z^*$.
This is a strong indicator of formulation quality, as it means the continuous
relaxation of our big-M model perfectly describes the integer hull of the
feasible region for this instance.

\paragraph{Aggregated vs.\ disaggregated capacity constraints.}
Our formulation uses \emph{aggregated} arc-capacity constraints~(C4):
\[
  \sum_{p \in P} x_a^p \;\leq\; \mathit{CapArc}_a \cdot z_a
  \qquad \forall\, a \in A_{\text{fixed}},
\]
which links the total cross-product flow to a single binary.
An alternative \emph{disaggregated} formulation would impose one constraint
per product:
\[
  x_a^p \;\leq\; \mathit{CapArc}_a \cdot z_a
  \qquad \forall\, p \in P,\; a \in A_{\text{fixed}},
\]
adding $|P| \cdot |A_{\text{fixed}}| = 3 \times 47 = 141$ constraints instead
of 47.
Disaggregated constraints are theoretically tighter because they bound each
product's individual flow rather than just their aggregate, producing a stronger
LP~relaxation in general.
In practice, however, since our aggregated formulation already achieves a 0\%
LP--IP gap, the disaggregated version offers no benefit here and would merely
increase model size.
We therefore retain the aggregated formulation for computational efficiency.

\begin{tcolorbox}[notebox, title={Phase~1 key finding}]
\small
The cost-minimising network concentrates 100\% of semiconductor supply
through a single hub~(H3, Singapore).
This structural dependency is cost-optimal under normal conditions but
creates a critical single point of failure under supply-disruption scenarios.
\end{tcolorbox}
